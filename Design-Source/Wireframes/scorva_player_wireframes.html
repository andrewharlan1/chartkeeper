Scorva — Player View Wireframes

Wireframe · v0.1 · Apr 30 2026
Wireframe-fidelity layouts for the player journey, drawn against the Scorva design system tokens (paper background, deep ink, warm orange accent, diff palette). The hero flow is the updated-part moment — three approaches to that moment are shown side by side. All other screens have one treatment each. Two form factors: iPad landscape and laptop. Inline notes flag assumptions and deferred behavior.

Design notes

Design notes

  • assume Top toolbar = full toolset; side toolbar = the same tools, mirrored. They're not redundant — one is dominant-hand, one is wrist-of-the-other-hand.

Trade-offs · A

  • Wins: Player sees the page immediately. Changes are visible in context. Reassurance ("7 preserved") is right next to the changed-count.
  • Loses: If they ignore the banner, they may miss what changed in measures that aren't on this page (m.34, m.42 are off-screen).
  • assume "Reviewed" dismisses the banner AND clears the per-measure highlight on subsequent opens. Until then, every open re-shows the banner.
  • "Jump to next change" is the bottom-right escape hatch for the off-page-changes problem. Cheap to implement.

05b · Updated part — Approach B: bottom-sheet summary

A modal-ish bottom sheet on first open. Three numbers (changed / preserved / review). The player picks "Show me" to dismiss the sheet and land in the inline-highlighted part, or "Skip" to dismiss without ceremony. Sheet only appears once per version; subsequent opens land directly in the inline state.

Trade-offs · B

  • Wins: The fear-question gets answered first ("preserved" is the largest, greenest number). Good for high-stakes scenarios where the player wants to feel the migration was clean before they trust the page.
  • Loses: Adds a ceremony step. For a small change (1 measure, no annotations affected) it's overkill. Could create "summary fatigue" if pushed often.
  • assume Sheet auto-suppresses for changes below a threshold (e.g., 0 changed measures + 0 affected annotations) — falls back to Approach A inline state.
  • assume "Settings · always summarize" lets a power user opt this on for every open. Default is "first open only."
  • "Needs your review" is the count of annotations whose migration confidence was below threshold. Feeds Screen 06.

05c · Updated part — Approach C: guided stepper

The part opens scrolled to the first changed measure with a callout pointing at it and a stepper at the bottom. The player walks through changes one at a time. "Done" exits to resting state. For high-stakes changes (chamber music with dense markings), this protects against missing things.

Trade-offs · C

  • Wins: Forces the player to acknowledge each change. The strongest reassurance design. Best for high-precision contexts (TAK Ensemble persona).
  • Loses: Most ceremony. Will feel paternalistic for jazz and worship players who just want to scan and play.
  • assume "Done" is also "I've reviewed" — same dismissal semantic as Approach A's "Reviewed" button.
  • The page already starts scrolled to the first changed measure — no separate "scroll to" affordance needed.
  • defer Per-segment user preference ("always step me through" vs "just banner") — a setting we'd add post-research if both are desired.
Recommendation
Take Approach A as the default with Approach B as an opt-in for first-opens-of-large-changes. Approach A respects the player's expertise — they can read music and a highlighted measure tells them what they need to know. The persistent banner does the reassurance work without ceremony. Reserve Approach B for cases where the change is large enough that "is this safe?" is genuinely uncertain (e.g., meter changes that re-anchor annotations, ≥10 measures changed, ≥3 annotations relocated). Approach C is the right design for educator workflows when those land — students benefit from being walked through.

  • defer Pen color/width controls are shown as toolbar pills but not designed in detail — a settings popover off each will arrive later.

07 · Auto-split at barlines

A behavior that needs to be designed honestly because it's surprising. The player draws a long ink stroke spanning measures 9–11. On auto-commit, the stroke splits at the barlines into three per-measure objects. A subtle one-time tooltip explains. After that, the split is silent — but the player can see it via hover on the bounding boxes (each is its own selectable object).

Design notes

  • The honest design choice: the split is a real behavior, not a visual abstraction. Showing it briefly (with the seam visible at the barline) is more trustworthy than hiding it.
  • assume The "Got it" tooltip shows once per user, not per draw.
  • After dismissal, hovering one of the three annotation segments highlights only that segment — visual confirmation that it's three separate objects.
  • This protects against the surprise moment when the user later tries to drag "the crescendo" and finds it's three things — they've been told what to expect.
  • defer A "treat as one" toggle for crescendos that should logically be one object. Listed under measure-span anchoring in the deferred doc.

09 · Version history

Tapping "see full version history" opens a Docs-style split: preview of the part at the selected version on the left, timeline rail on the right. Read-only — viewing past versions doesn't push annotations to them. Every entry says what changed in that version of THIS part (history is per-part, not per-chart).

Design notes

  • assume Per-part history is the right framing. Showing the whole-chart history would be overwhelming and the player only cares about THEIR part anyway.
  • The preview on the left renders the part at the selected version. Read-only mode is signaled by the topbar pill.
  • "Open at v3" lets the player jump back to the live editing experience at the current version.
  • v1 is a "ghost" entry because the player's part didn't exist yet — explicit, not silenced.
  • defer Side-by-side compare ("v2 vs v3 of this part") — would live as a tertiary action on a history entry.
